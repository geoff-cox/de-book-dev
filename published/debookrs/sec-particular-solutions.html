    Algebra.

    When given an algebraic equation such as \(x^3 - 6x = 4\text{,}\) the solution is all numbers, \(x\text{,}\) that satisfy the equation. One such solution is \(x=-2\text{.}\) This can be verified by plugging \(x=-2\) into the equation. After simplifying, if the value on the left equals the value on the right, the solution must be valid. When this happens, we say that the solution "satisfies" this equation.
    \begin{align*} x^3 - 6x \amp = 4 \\ \left(-2\right)^3 - 6\left(-2\right) \amp = 4 \\ -8 + 12 \amp = 4 \\ 4 \amp = 4 \, \checkmark \end{align*}
    The number, \(x=-2\text{,}\) is a solution since it "satisfies" this equation.
    In contrast, when solving a differential equation such as \(y' - 6y = 10x - 5\text{,}\) we are looking for all functions, \(y\text{,}\) that satisfy the equation. One solution is \(y = -5x\) and we can verify it in the same way.
    \begin{align*} y' - 2y \amp = 10x - 5 \\ \left(-5x\right)^{\prime} - 2\left(-5x\right) \amp = 10x - 5 \\ -5 + 10x \amp = 10x - 5 \\ 10x - 5 \amp = 10x - 5 \, \checkmark \end{align*}
    The function, \(y = -5x\text{,}\) is a solution since it "satisfies" this equation.

    An Analogy.

    It is helpful to make an analogy here by thinking about what it means to be a solution to an algebraic equation. Consider (but don’t solve!) the following problem and think about the questions that follow.
    What does it mean when we say \(x = -1\) is a solution to the equation below?
    \begin{equation*} x^5 + 12x^2 +10x = 7x^3 + 24 \end{equation*}

    Question 17. What does solution mean in this case?

    In the context of this equation, a solution would be an \(x\)-value that "works" when we substitute it back into the equation (i.e. it satisfies the equation). If we substitute \(x = -1\) into each side of the equation, we have
    \begin{align*} \mbox{left hand side of the equation (LHS)} %\amp= x^5 + 12x^2 +10x,\\ \amp= (-1)^5 + 12(-1)^2 +10(-1),\\ \amp= -1 + 12 - 11,\\ \amp= 0, \end{align*}
    and
    \begin{align*} \mbox{right hand side of the equation (RHS)} %\amp= 7x^3 + 24,\\ \amp= 7(-1)^3 + 24,\\ \amp= -7 + 24,\\ \amp= 17. \end{align*}
    When we substitute \(x = -1\) into the left hand side of the equation we get \(0\text{,}\) whereas we get \(17\) on the right hand side. This means \(x = -1\) isn’t a solution to the algebraic equation. However, if we try \(x = -3,\) we have
    \begin{align*} \mbox{LHS} %\amp= x^5 + 12x^2 +10x,\\ \amp= (-3)^5 + 12(-3)^2 +10(-3),\\ \amp= -243 + 108 - 30,\\ \amp= -165, \end{align*}
    and
    \begin{align*} \mbox{RHS} %\amp= 7x^3 + 24,\\ \amp= 7(-3)^3 + 24,\\ \amp= -189 + 24,\\ \amp= -165. \end{align*}
    Since we get the same result when we substitute \(x = -3\) in on the left hand and right hand sides of the equation, we can conclude that \(x = -3\) is a solution to the algebraic equation.
    Let’s take a moment to highlight some important details about how the previous example gives insight into the concept of a solution to a differential equation.
    • Your intuition for algebra should tell you that there is probably more solutions than \(x = -3\) to this equation (and you’d be correct). It turns out that differential equations also can have multiple solutions!
    • At no point did we discuss the technique needed to find the solution \(x = -3\text{.}\) However, this did not stop us from verifying that it was a solution. This fact also transfers to differential equations! Indeed, as you will see in the exercises, given a function, you will be able to verify whether this function is (or is not) a solution to a differential equation.
    We have just discussed some important ideas that will aid us in understanding what it means to be a solution to an differential equation. You will see that the next two statement are practically identical (except the blue terms).
    • \(\color{blue}{x}\) (a \(\color{blue}{\text{number}}\)) is a solution to an \(\color{blue}{\text{algebraic}}\) equation if we get the same result on the left and right-hand side of the equation after substituting \(\color{blue}{x}\) into the entire equation.
    • \(\color{blue}{y}\) (a \(\color{blue}{\text{function}}\)) is a solution to a \(\color{blue}{\text{differential}}\) equation if we get the same result on the left and right-hand side of the equation after substituting \(\color{blue}{y}\) into the entire equation.
    In all of the previous examples, we verified that one particular function was a solution to the DE, but as previously mentioned, we know that there may be more. It turns out, differential equations generally have infinitely many solutions. We can use Example 25 to illustrate this.
    In this example, we showed that the function \(y = c_1e^{-3t} + c_2e^{4t}\) was a solution \(y'' - y' - 12y = 0\text{.}\)
    Notice that we were able to verify the solution, even though the constants, \(c_1\) and \(c_2\text{,}\) were not known. This suggests that you can substitute any values of \(c_1\) and \(c_2\) into the solution and it will still be a solution to the DE. Try it!
    Now, since there are infinitely many choices for \(c_1\) and \(c_2\text{,}\) then, logically, there must be infinitely many solutions to the DE, \(y'' - y' - 12y = 0\text{.}\)

    Definition 18.

    Given a differential equation, the collection of all solutions of this DE is called the family of solutions of this DE. The form of these solutions is called the general solution of this DE. A single solution from the family of solutions is called a particular solution.

    Remark 19.

    A family of solutions can be viewed as the collection of all particular solutions. Conversely, a particular solution can be viewed as a member of the family of solutions.
    In the context of Example 25, all of the following would be true:
    • \(y = c_1e^{-3t} + c_2e^{4t}\) is the solution to \(y'' - y' - 12y = 0\text{.}\)
    • \(y = c_1e^{-3t} + c_2e^{4t}\) is the general solution to the \(y'' - y' - 12y = 0\text{.}\)
    • Any solution that has the form: \(y = c_1e^{-3t} + c_2e^{4t}\) is in the family of solutions for \(y'' - y' - 12y = 0\text{.}\)
    • \(y = -e^{-3t} + 7e^{4t}\) is a particular solution to \(y'' - y' - 12y = 0 \,\,\) (\(c_1 = -1\text{,}\) \(c_2 = 7\)).
    • \(y = -e^{-3t} + 7e^{4t}\) is in the family of solutions for \(y'' - y' - 12y = 0\) since it has the form: \(y = c_1e^{-3t} + c_2e^{4t}\) where \(c_1 = -1\) and \(c_2 = 7\text{.}\)

    Remark 20.

    When working on your own problems, remember that the following three statements are referring to the exact same thing.
    • Solve the following DE.
    • Find the general solution to the following DE.
    • Find the family of solutions for the following DE.
    We spend a lot of time solving equations, but do you even remember what it means to be a solution? Let’s review what it means to be a solution to an algebraic equation (as in question (4)), and then what it means to be a solution to a differential equation (as in question (5)), and then we’ll extend those ideas in questions (6) and (7).
    1. Verify that \(x = 3\) is a solution to the equation
      \begin{equation} 2x^2 + 3 = 7x.\tag{4} \end{equation}
      What confirms that \(x = 3\) is a solution?
      Answer.
      the LHS = RHS after substituting \(x = 3\) into both sides of the equation.
      (Note: Do not rearrange the equation to solve for \(x\text{.}\) That would be the process of finding a solution; you are asked to verify a solution.)
      Solution.
      We substitute \(x = 3\) into the left hand side (LHS) of the equation, and then into the right hand side (RHS) of the equation:
      \begin{align*} \text{LHS} \amp= 2x^2 + 3 \\ \amp= 2(3)^2 + 3 \\ \amp= 18 + 3 \\ \amp= 21 \\ \amp \\ \text{RHS} \amp= 7x \\ \amp= 7(3) \\ \amp= 21 \end{align*}
      Since we get the same result when we substitute the solution into the RHS and into the LHS, we have confirmed that \(x = 3\) is a solution to the equation.
    2. Verify that \(y = c_1 \sin x + c_2 \cos x\) is a solution to the DE
      \begin{equation} \DS \frac{d^2y}{dx^2} + y = 0.\tag{5} \end{equation}
      Hint.
      Look back at the previous question and think about what it means to be a solution to a DE.
      Solution.
      We need to substitute into the DE. In order to substitute into the left hand side, we need to know the second derivative. So we begin by finding that.
      \begin{align*} y \amp= c_1 \sin x + c_2 \cos x \\ \Rightarrow \quad y' \amp= c_1 \cos x - c_2 \sin x \\ \Rightarrow \quad y'' \amp= -c_1 \sin x - c_2 \cos x \end{align*}
      Now we can substitute into the DE:
      \begin{align*} \text{LHS}\amp= \frac{d^2y}{dx^2} + y \\ \amp= \Big[ -c_1 \sin x - c_2 \cos x \Big] + \Big[ c_1 \sin x + c_2 \cos x \Big] \\ \amp= 0 \\ \amp= \text{RHS} \end{align*}
      Since when we substitute in to the DE, we find that the LHS is equal to the RHS, we have verified that \(y = c_1 \sin x + c_2 \cos x\) is a solution to the DE \(\DS \frac{d^2y}{dx^2} + y = 0.\)
      Answer.
      Since we get the same result (0) when we substitute the solution into the RHS and into the LHS, we confirm that \(y = c_1 \sin x + c_2 \cos x\) is a solution to the DE.
    3. Consider the DE
      \begin{equation} \DS \frac{d^2 y}{dx^2} + 6 \frac{dy}{dx} + 5y = 0.\tag{6} \end{equation}
      Find all values of \(m\) such that \(y = e^{mx}\) is a solution to the DE by taking derivatives and substituting into the DE, and then solving for \(m\text{.}\)
      Hint.
      \(e^{mx} \ne 0,\) no matter the value of \(x\) or \(m.\) Therefore we might want to factor it out as it won’t contribute to our answer.
      Solution.
      First we take derivatives so that we can substitute into the DE.
      \begin{align*} y \amp= e^{mx} \\ \Rightarrow \quad y' \amp= me^{mx} \\ \Rightarrow \quad y'' \amp= m^2 e^{mx} \end{align*}
      Now we can substitute into the DE and solve for \(m\text{:}\)
      \begin{align*} \frac{d^2 y}{dx^2} + 6 \frac{dy}{dx} + 5y \amp= 0 \\ \Big[ m^2 e^{mx} \Big] + 6\Big[ m e^{mx} \Big] + 5\Big[ e^{mx} \Big] \amp= 0 \\ e^{mx} \Big[ m^2 + 6m + 5 Big] \amp= 0 \\ e^{mx} (m+5)(m+1) \amp= 0 \end{align*}
      Recall that \(e^{mx} \ne 0,\) no matter the value of \(x\) or \(m.\) Thus we have:
      \begin{align*} m+5 = 0 \amp \text{ or } m+1 = 0 \\ m = -5, \amp \text{ or } m = -1 \end{align*}
      So the two solutions are \(m = -5, -1.\)
      Answer.
      \begin{equation*} m = -5, -1 \end{equation*}
    4. Now that you have (hopefully!) two values for \(m,\) choose one of the two values and then verify that \(y = e^{mx}\) is a solution for that value of \(m.\)
      Solution.
      The work here is for \(m = -5,\) and the work for \(m = -1\) is similar.
      First we take derivatives so that we can substitute into the DE.
      \begin{align*} y \amp= e^{-5x} \\ \Rightarrow \quad y' \amp= -5e^{-5x} \\ \Rightarrow \quad y'' \amp= 25 e^{-5x} \end{align*}
      Now we can substitute into the DE:
      \begin{align*} \text{LHS}\amp= \frac{d^2 y}{dx^2} + 6 \frac{dy}{dx} + 5y \\ \amp= \Big[ 25e^{-5x} \Big] + 6\Big[ -5e^{-5x} \Big] + 5\Big[ e^{-5x} \Big] \\ \amp= 25e^{-5x} - 30 e^{-5x} + 5e^{-5x} \\ \amp= 0 \\ \amp= \text{RHS} \end{align*}
      Hence, we have shown that \(y = e^{-5x}\) is a solution to the DE \(\DS \frac{d^2 y}{dx^2} + 6 \frac{dy}{dx} + 5y = 0.\)
      Answer.
      Since we get the same result (0) when we substitute the solution into the RHS and into the LHS, we confirm that \(y = e^{-5x}\) (or \(y = e^{-x}\)) is a solution to the DE.
    5. Find all values of \(m\) such that \(y = x^m\) is a solution of the DE
      \begin{equation} \DS 3x^2 \frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = -11x \frac{dy}{dx} + 3y.\tag{7} \end{equation}
      Solution.
      We proceed as in the previous question. First we take derivatives so that we can substitute into the DE.
      \begin{align*} y \amp= x^m \\ \Rightarrow \quad y' \amp= mx^{m-1} \\ \Rightarrow \quad y'' \amp= m(m-1)x^{m-2} \end{align*}
      Now we can substitute into the DE:
      \begin{align*} 3x^2 \frac{d^2y}{dx^2} \amp= -11x \frac{dy}{dx} + 3y \\ 3x^2\Big[ m(m-1)x^{m-2} \Big] \amp= -11x\Big[ mx^{m-1} \Big] + 3\Big[ x^m \Big] \\ 3m(m-1)x^2 x^{m-2} \amp= -11mx^1\cdot x^{m-1} + 3x^m \\ (3m^2 - 3m)x^{2+(m-2)} \amp= -11m x^{1 + (m-1)} + 3x^m \\ (3m^2 - 3m)x^{m} \amp= -11m x^{m} + 3x^m \\ (3m^2 - 3m)x^{m} + 11m x^{m} - 3x^m \amp= 0 \\ (3m^2 - 3m + 11m - 3)x^m \amp= 0 \\ (3m^2 +8m - 3)x^m \amp= 0 \\ (3m -1)(m+3)x^m \amp= 0 \end{align*}
      Note that there is no value of \(m\) that can guarantee that \(x^m\) is 0. Hence we have
      \begin{align*} 3m-1 = 0 \amp \quad\text{or}\quad m+3 = 0 \\ m = \frac{1}{3}, \amp \quad\text{or}\quad m = -3 \end{align*}
      So the two solutions are \(m = -3, \frac{1}{3}.\)
      Answer.
      \begin{equation*} m = -3, \frac{1}{3} \end{equation*}
