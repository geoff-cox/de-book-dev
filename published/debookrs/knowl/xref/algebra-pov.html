Algebra.

When given an algebraic equation such as \(x^3 - 6x = 4\text{,}\) the solution is all numbers, \(x\text{,}\) that satisfy the equation. One such solution is \(x=-2\text{.}\) This can be verified by plugging \(x=-2\) into the equation. After simplifying, if the value on the left equals the value on the right, the solution must be valid. When this happens, we say that the solution "satisfies" this equation.
\begin{align*} x^3 - 6x \amp = 4 \\ \left(-2\right)^3 - 6\left(-2\right) \amp = 4 \\ -8 + 12 \amp = 4 \\ 4 \amp = 4 \, \checkmark \end{align*}
The number, \(x=-2\text{,}\) is a solution since it "satisfies" this equation.
In contrast, when solving a differential equation such as \(y' - 6y = 10x - 5\text{,}\) we are looking for all functions, \(y\text{,}\) that satisfy the equation. One solution is \(y = -5x\) and we can verify it in the same way.
\begin{align*} y' - 2y \amp = 10x - 5 \\ \left(-5x\right)^{\prime} - 2\left(-5x\right) \amp = 10x - 5 \\ -5 + 10x \amp = 10x - 5 \\ 10x - 5 \amp = 10x - 5 \, \checkmark \end{align*}
The function, \(y = -5x\text{,}\) is a solution since it "satisfies" this equation.
